Who am I?

I am currently enrolled in a Second Bachelor's degree program for Computer Science at Univeristy at Albany. I graduated last may from Morrisville State College with a 3.88GPA and aBachelor's of Technology degree in Application Software Development. I am a SUNY Chancellor's Award winner as well as a member of ODK and PTK.

What am I doing in Albany?

Morrisville's program was essentially a trade school for software development. You learned how to copy code and paste things together, but you never really learned how it all worked. I wanted to get into programming at a lower level so I planned to obtain a graduate degree in Computer Science. However, first I needed to make up a lot of math and basic Comp Sci courses. Initially I was told this would take about 1-1.5 years to complete. Upon scheduling all my classes I was given a new estimate of 2-2.5 years. This being too long for my liking I accepted a job offer for after the Spring semester where I can work to pay off my student loans as well as have my employer pay for graduate classes.

Why take such an unusual path?

The above only describes the crucial portion of my awkward times as a college student. With my current resume I have been given many job offers, some of which were above what my fellow Morrisville graduates could expect as a recent graduate. Many have asked why I didn't just accept one of the offers when they were given to me? Easy, I wanted more, I wanted better. I didn't want to be a code monkey like those around me. So I worked hard and took a risk that paid off. I have landed a great job with awesome pay and very, very cool toys to work with ^.^

Why take a web design class?

I have always been curious about web technologies (and web design in paticular) as it wasn't a part of my ciriculum at Morrisville. I have also lost out on many opportunities to make some extra cash from people wanting some sites developed. In addition the interesting possibilites of HTML5 have piqued my curiosity and I wanted to play around with its new features.
